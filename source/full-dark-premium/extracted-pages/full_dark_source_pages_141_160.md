Direct Stigma and the Spiral 
Direct stigma is when someone looks at your symptoms, your coping, your collapse, your 
addiction, your fear, your lateness, your freeze, your panic, and calls it a personal defect. 
Not pain. 
Not injury. 
Not survival. 
Defect. 
It sounds like: 
Lazy. 
Weak. 
Crazy. 
Selfish. 
Manipulative. 
Immature. 
Toxic. 
Hopeless. 
Nobody. 
When those words come from strangers, they hurt. 
When they come from people close to you, they brand.

Because then you stop fighting them. 
You start agreeing. 
Maybe I am lazy.

Old coping leads to damage. 
Damage leads to more shame. 
More shame leads to more hiding. 
Around and around until the second wound becomes infected. 
And nobody wants to talk about that part because it is easier to blame the person bleeding. 
Indirect Stigma 
Direct stigma was not the only thing that got me. 
Indirect stigma shaped me too. 
That is the kind you absorb without anyone aiming it directly at you. 
TV jokes. 
Small-town gossip.

Stigma and Addiction 
Stigma around addiction is especially cruel because it attacks the exact place recovery needs to 
start: honesty. 
Recovery requires truth. 
I’m using. 
I’m craving. 
I’m scared. 
I’m ashamed. 
I need help. 
I’m close to relapse. 
I don’t trust myself tonight. 
But stigma makes those sentences feel dangerous. 
Because once you say them, people may stop seeing you as human and start seeing you as a 
category. 
Addict. 
Junkie. 
Meth head. 
Dope fiend. 
Problem. 
Risk. 
Failure. 
So you lie. 
You minimize. 
You hide. 
You say you’re fine when you are already halfway gone. 
Then when relapse happens, people say: 
“Why didn’t you tell anyone?” 
Because shame was standing at the door with a gun.

Literally. 
It keeps people from asking for help until the fire is already through the roof.

I am not saying addiction should have no consequences.

Damage is real. 
Trust has to be rebuilt with behavior, not speeches.

Humiliation is not treatment. 
Disgust is not a recovery plan. 
If you want someone to step out of hiding, you cannot make the truth more dangerous than the 
lie. 
That is the math. 
And for most of my life, the truth felt dangerous. 
So I hid. 
Stigma and the Legal System 
The legal system did not create my trauma, but it collided with it hard. 
Missed court dates. 
Failures to comply.

For most of my life, I treated my body like the enemy. 
Too anxious. 
Too tired. 
Too reactive. 
Too needy. 
Too weak. 
Too loud. 
Too hungry for relief. 
Too broken to trust. 
I thought if I could just overpower it, shame it, medicate it, numb it, work it harder, punish it, or 
ignore it long enough, maybe it would finally shut up. 
It never did. 
Because the body was not trying to ruin me. 
It was trying to protect me with outdated instructions. 
That is the tragedy of trauma physiology. 
The body keeps running old survival code even after the original danger is gone. 
It keeps scanning. 
Bracing. 
Freezing. 
Fawning. 
Reaching. 
Hiding. 
Preparing. 
Not because it hates you. 
Because it never got the message that the war ended.

People love to talk about mindset. 
Change your thoughts. 
Think positive. 
Decide better. 
Choose differently. 
And yes, thoughts matter. 
Beliefs matter. 
Choices matter. 
But if the body is in alarm, mindset has to fight biology. 
You can tell yourself you are safe while your heart is trying to run from a room. 
You can tell yourself not to panic while your lungs forget how to breathe. 
You can tell yourself not to use while every cell is screaming for relief. 
You can tell yourself to go to court while your legs feel welded to the floor. 
You can tell yourself to answer the phone while your throat locks around the words. 
That does not mean the body wins every time. 
But it means the body must be part of the work. 
Ignoring it is not strength. 
It is negligence. 
For me, healing had to move from confession into embodiment. 
Not just: 
What happened? 
But: 
Where does it live?
